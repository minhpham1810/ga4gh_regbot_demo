GA4GH Framework for Responsible Sharing of Genomic and Health-Related Data
[SAMPLE SEED FILE — for prototyping only, not an official GA4GH publication]

1. Purpose

This Framework establishes the foundational principles and obligations for the responsible
sharing of genomic and health-related data across international jurisdictions. It applies
to all participants in the GA4GH ecosystem, including data contributors, custodians,
access committees, and data users.

The Framework recognises that sharing genomic data promotes scientific discovery and
clinical benefit, while acknowledging that such data carries inherent privacy risks for
individuals and their biological relatives.

2. Interpretation

2.1 Definitions

"Data" means any genomic, phenotypic, clinical, or health-related information pertaining
to an identifiable or potentially identifiable individual.

"Data Contributor" means any individual, institution, or organisation that deposits data
into a sharing infrastructure governed by this Framework.

"Data User" means any individual, institution, or organisation that applies for and
receives access to data under this Framework.

"Data Access Committee (DAC)" means the body responsible for reviewing and adjudicating
data access requests in accordance with the applicable data use conditions.

"Data Use Ontology (DUO)" means the machine-readable ontology that encodes the permitted
uses of a dataset, enabling automated compatibility checking.

2.2 Interpretation

Unless context otherwise requires, terms defined in Section 2.1 shall bear the same
meaning wherever used in this Framework.

3. Principles of Responsible Data Sharing

3.1 Respect for Persons
Data sharing must respect the autonomy, privacy, and dignity of data contributors and
research participants. Informed consent processes must be meaningful, comprehensible,
and aligned with the purposes for which data will be used.

3.2 Beneficence
Data sharing activities must be designed to produce scientific or clinical benefit.
Requests for access must demonstrate a legitimate research purpose and a clear plan
for translating findings into benefit.

3.3 Justice
Benefits from data sharing must be distributed equitably. Access committees must ensure
that underrepresented populations are not disproportionately burdened or excluded from
benefit.

3.4 Non-maleficence
Data sharing must not expose participants or communities to harm. Risk assessments must
be conducted prior to data release, and access controls must be proportional to risk.

4. Data Access Controls

4.1 Tiered Access Framework
Data may be made available under one of three tiers:
  (a) Open Access — no restrictions; typically applies to aggregated or fully
      de-identified summary statistics.
  (b) Registered Access — requires user registration and agreement to terms of use;
      applies to pseudonymised data with low re-identification risk.
  (c) Controlled Access — requires formal application to a Data Access Committee;
      applies to individual-level genomic data.

4.2 Data Access Committee Obligations
Each DAC must:
  (i)   operate according to a documented review process;
  (ii)  publish its review criteria;
  (iii) reach decisions within a reasonable and published timeframe;
  (iv)  maintain records of decisions for audit purposes.

4.3 Data Use Agreements
All controlled-access users must execute a Data Use Agreement (DUA) that specifies:
  - the permitted purpose(s);
  - the duration of access;
  - security and handling requirements;
  - obligations regarding publication and acknowledgment;
  - consequences of non-compliance.

5. Consent and Participant Autonomy

5.1 Broad Consent
Where permitted by law and ethics frameworks, broad consent may be used to cover
future, unspecified research uses, provided that:
  (a) participants are informed that data may be shared beyond the original study;
  (b) ongoing governance structures exist to review new uses;
  (c) participants retain the right to withdraw consent.

5.2 Dynamic Consent
Dynamic consent platforms that allow participants to modify their consent preferences
over time are encouraged where technically feasible.

5.3 Withdrawal
Data contributors must honour participant withdrawal requests. Derived data that cannot
be disaggregated from aggregated analyses need not be deleted, but no further individual-
level access should be permitted post-withdrawal.

6. Data Security and Privacy

6.1 Minimum Necessary Data
Data access should be limited to the minimum data necessary to achieve the stated
research purpose. Requests for unrestricted bulk data should be scrutinised carefully.

6.2 Technical Safeguards
Data custodians must implement:
  - access control mechanisms (authentication and authorisation);
  - encryption of data at rest and in transit;
  - audit logging of data access events;
  - incident response procedures.

6.3 De-identification and Re-identification Risk
Data should be de-identified to the maximum degree consistent with research utility.
Custodians must conduct periodic re-identification risk assessments and apply
additional protections as risk increases.

7. Cross-Border Data Transfer

7.1 Jurisdiction Compatibility
Cross-border data transfers must comply with the laws of both the source and destination
jurisdictions. Where legal conflicts arise, the more protective standard applies.

7.2 Adequacy and Safeguards
In the absence of a formal adequacy determination, appropriate safeguards must be in
place before data leaves the source jurisdiction, including:
  - standard contractual clauses;
  - binding corporate rules where applicable;
  - explicit participant consent for the specific transfer.

7.3 Federated Analysis
Where legally and technically feasible, federated analysis approaches — where computation
is brought to the data rather than data to the computation — are preferred for cross-
border scenarios.

8. Researcher Obligations

8.1 Compliance with DUA Terms
Data users must comply fully with all terms of the Data Use Agreement, including
restrictions on secondary use, sub-licensing, and re-identification.

8.2 Publication and Attribution
Users must acknowledge the data source and contributing cohort(s) in all publications
and presentations arising from use of the shared data.

8.3 Reporting Incidental Findings
Where clinically actionable incidental findings arise during research, users must follow
the applicable policy for disclosure, as defined in the DUA or ethics approval.

9. Governance and Accountability

9.1 Institutional Responsibility
Institutions that allow data use under this Framework bear responsibility for ensuring
that individual researchers comply with its requirements.

9.2 Sanctions
Violations of the Framework may result in:
  - suspension or revocation of data access;
  - notification to the researcher's institution;
  - reporting to relevant regulatory authorities.

9.3 Review and Amendment
This Framework shall be reviewed no less than every three years. Amendments require
consensus among the GA4GH membership.
